MRI Arthrogram

The MRI Arthrogram procedure begins with a local anesthetic to numb the area, followed by the injection of a contrast agent into the joint. The contrast agent helps to enhance the visibility of the joint structures, such as the cartilage, ligaments, and tendons, during the MRI scan. Once the contrast is administered, the patient is positioned in the MRI machine, and detailed images of the joint are captured from multiple angles. The procedure typically takes 30-60 minutes, and patients may be asked to remain still during the scan to ensure clear and accurate images. Afterward, patients may experience mild discomfort or swelling at the injection site, but these symptoms usually subside within a few days. The results of the MRI Arthrogram are reviewed by a radiologist, who will provide the physician with a detailed report to assist in diagnosing joint-related conditions. If necessary, follow-up imaging or treatments may be recommended based on the findings.

The MRI Arthrogram procedure begins by first administering a local anesthetic to numb the area around the joint being examined. This is done to ensure that the patient remains comfortable throughout the procedure. Once the area is numb, a contrast agent, typically a dye containing gadolinium, is injected directly into the joint space using a needle. The contrast agent helps to provide better visibility of the joint structures during the MRI scan by highlighting areas such as cartilage, tendons, ligaments, and the surrounding soft tissues. The contrast makes it easier to detect abnormalities such as tears, inflammation, or degeneration in the joint.

After the contrast is injected, the patient is positioned on the MRI table, and the MRI machine is used to capture detailed images of the joint from multiple angles. The MRI scan uses strong magnetic fields and radio waves to generate high-resolution images that allow healthcare providers to assess the internal structures of the joint with precision. The procedure typically lasts between 30 and 60 minutes, during which the patient must remain still to ensure that the images are clear and free from blurring. The MRI machine may be noisy during the scan, but patients are usually given earplugs to reduce discomfort.

After the scan is completed, the patient may experience some mild discomfort, swelling, or bruising at the injection site, though these symptoms generally subside within a few days. The contrast agent used is generally safe, but in rare cases, some patients may experience an allergic reaction. If this happens, the medical team will provide appropriate treatment. It is also recommended that patients avoid strenuous activities or heavy lifting for a short period after the procedure to allow the joint to recover from the injection.

The MRI Arthrogram images are reviewed by a radiologist, who will analyze the detailed joint images for signs of injury, inflammation, or other abnormalities. The radiologist’s report is then sent to the referring physician, who will use the results to help diagnose the patient’s condition and determine the best course of treatment. In cases of significant findings, such as joint tears or cartilage damage, further treatment options, such as surgery, physical therapy, or medications, may be recommended. Follow-up imaging may also be needed to track the progress of the condition or evaluate the effectiveness of treatment.

The MRI Arthrogram procedure begins with a local anesthetic to numb the area, followed by the injection of a contrast agent into the joint. Once the contrast is administered, an MRI scan is performed to capture detailed images of the joint.

The MRI Arthrogram procedure utilizes an MRI machine, a contrast agent (such as gadolinium), and specialized needles for the joint injection. The MRI machine creates detailed images of the joint and surrounding structures, while the contrast agent enhances the images.

MRI Arthrogram provides detailed, high-resolution images that allow doctors to diagnose joint problems such as tears, inflammation, or structural abnormalities with great accuracy. It is particularly helpful when standard MRI scans do not provide sufficient information for diagnosis.
