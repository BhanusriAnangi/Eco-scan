Ultrasound Elastography

Ultrasound Elastography is a diagnostic imaging technique that measures the stiffness of tissues in the body, providing valuable information about liver fibrosis, tumors, and other conditions.

Description

Ultrasound Elastography is an advanced imaging technique that uses ultrasound waves to measure the stiffness of tissues in the body. It is a non-invasive procedure commonly used to assess liver stiffness in patients with liver diseases like cirrhosis or fibrosis. It can also be used to evaluate the stiffness of other tissues, such as tumors, to help guide treatment decisions. Unlike biopsy, elastography provides real-time, reliable data without the need for tissue sampling.

The Ultrasound Elastography procedure involves applying an ultrasound gel to the skin, followed by a probe that transmits sound waves into the body. The stiffness of the tissue is then measured based on the speed of the sound waves. A small amount of pressure is applied to the skin, but the procedure is non-invasive and painless. It typically takes about 10-15 minutes to complete.

The primary equipment used in Ultrasound Elastography includes:

  • Ultrasound Machine: A specialized ultrasound machine that emits sound waves and captures the tissue's response to those waves.
  • Elastography Probe: A probe that measures the stiffness of tissues by assessing the velocity of sound waves as they pass through the tissue.

Ultrasound Elastography offers several benefits to patients, including:

  • Non-invasive: The procedure does not require surgery or a biopsy, making it safer and more comfortable for patients.
  • Quick and Painless: It is a quick, painless procedure with no recovery time required.
  • Accurate Diagnosis: It provides reliable data to assess liver fibrosis, tumors, and other tissue abnormalities, allowing for early intervention.
  • No Radiation: Unlike some other imaging techniques, Elastography does not involve exposure to ionizing radiation.
