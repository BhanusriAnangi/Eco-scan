Arterial Embolization

Arterial Embolization is a minimally invasive procedure used to block blood flow to specific areas of the body, such as tumors, bleeding sites, or other abnormal blood vessels. It is commonly used to treat conditions like uterine fibroids, certain types of cancers, and to control severe bleeding. By blocking the blood supply, this procedure helps to reduce the size of tumors, manage bleeding, and improve patient outcomes without the need for traditional surgery. Arterial Embolization can also be used as a preparatory treatment before surgery, making it a crucial tool in modern interventional radiology. IR procedures are used in a variety of clinical situations, such as the treatment of tumors, controlling bleeding, removing kidney stones, and even performing biopsies to diagnose cancers or infections.

Description

Arterial Embolization involves the introduction of small particles or agents through a catheter to block specific blood vessels. This procedure is done under local anesthesia, often guided by fluoroscopy (real-time X-ray imaging), allowing the interventional radiologist to target the precise area of concern. It is commonly used to treat conditions such as cancer, fibroids, or vascular malformations, where the aim is to restrict blood flow to the affected area.

The procedure is minimally invasive, typically requiring only small incisions, and is an effective alternative to more invasive surgical options. It allows for quick recovery with minimal risk and provides a significant reduction in symptoms or tumor size. Embolization can also help control uncontrollable bleeding, which may occur during trauma or surgery.

Arterial Embolization involves the insertion of a catheter into an artery, usually through the groin or wrist. The catheter is navigated to the targeted blood vessel, and embolic agents such as tiny beads, coils, or foam are injected to block the blood flow. This procedure is carefully monitored with imaging technology to ensure the correct location is targeted.

The procedure uses a catheterization system, fluoroscopy for real-time imaging, and embolic agents like microspheres, coils, or gel foam. These tools enable precise delivery of the embolic material to the targeted blood vessels, minimizing damage to surrounding healthy tissues.

Arterial Embolization offers significant benefits including minimal invasiveness, quicker recovery time, reduced risk compared to traditional surgery, and the ability to treat conditions that might otherwise require extensive surgical procedures. It is also an effective method for controlling bleeding in trauma or surgical settings.
